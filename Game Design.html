Game Design for SlimeClimb & General Philosophy

Background Information

SlimeClimb is a 2D action platformer avaliable on iOS, Android & Steam; the objective is to climb as high as possible.

For the case of this portfolio I'll only be covering the main version of the game, which is the mobile version, a portrait only game.

To summarize simple mechanics of SlimeClimb, the player can jump, backflip, stick to walls, explode off the ground, and can die with a goal to climb as high as possible.


Project Information:

  • Genre: 2D vertical action platformer with physics-based movement
  • Platform Focus: Mobile-first (portrait orientation), designed for touchscreen input

Game Design Philosophy

SlimeClimb's distinctive visual style and gameplay.

Catching Eyes & Being Distinct

To get any game out I fully believe that you need to catch eyes and be distinct. I've seen so many posts of people trying to release their indie game without anything that's catching eyes, whether it's branding or just the game itself. For SlimeClimb, I'm really happy with how the game's look and style just works. When I make anything now, more than anything I want it to be distinct over anything.

Setting the Tone Instantly

From as soon as you open the game, the UI is dynamic, fluid and is over a pink backdrop that instantly sets the tone. The name is everywhere and you can't miss the characters, which drills in the branding of SlimeClimb. Color is everything,when you look at SlimeClimb you see pinks, lilacs, and purples.

Smart Design for Promotion

The result of designing smart like this is that every time I or a player goes to make a post, it's eye catching and instantly tells whoever is watching what the game is called. It's easy to promote and recognize since the color palette is already established.

SlimeClimb's bold branding and UI set the tone from the first screen.

Marketable, Memorable Characters

The characters are all marketable and have simple designs that are easy to remember and fit into the world. I was heavily inspired by games like Minecraft that have every silhouette and design so easy to recognize. Down the line, I want to be able to release plushies and merchandise,which would be really cool to have sitting on my desk and also super easy since the design is already merch ready in a sense.

Gameplay Design: Mechanics & Discovery

One-finger controls make SlimeClimb accessible, but mastery takes skill.

Fun Comes First

Gameplay design comes first imo since almost every game that is successful is inherently fun. For the gameplay I wanted it to be player focused and open to skill improvement. When you play SlimeClimb all you need to do is tap with one finger to jump and climb walls,super easy to understand and opens the game up to anyone from age 5 to 100.

Peeling Back the Mechanics

  • As you keep playing, you can peel back the mechanics and find better ways to climb using what you're given.
  • For example, you can backflip,sometimes it's more fun to have that fail safe to fall back on.
  • If you use it more than 6 in a row, you get an explosion where if you hit the ground it bounces you up. In the game it's never mentioned to the player that this is a thing; instead, I wanted this to be a natural discovery from spamming backflip or just on accident.

Discovery Over Explanation

If the game explained every mechanic and feature, I feel like it makes things less special sometimes. Games like Elden Ring work so well because of how fun it is to explore and naturally find out those mechanics and features. (Who else opened the tp chest and was terrified of the crystal cave lmao)

Replayability & Emergent Play

  • Speedrunners have made use of these more niche mechanics, which is kinda cool.
  • For example, in the game there is a strat where you can get an extra boost if you tap as soon as you hit a bounce pad,my beta testers found this!
  • Stuff like that really helps build some interesting ways to keep the game replayable.

Designing the Sandbox: Objects, Obstacles, and Emergent Play

SlimeClimb's world is a true sandbox, built from a simple set of player actions and expanded into a playground of interactive objects and challenges.

A map packed with interactive elements: vines, Box Jellies, bouncy walls, coins, and more.

Building Blocks of Play

  • Platforms to jump on
  • Walls to stick and slide on
  • Hazards and objects that can kill the player
  • Bouncy floors, auto-climb walls, spinning/moving/breakable walls, and interactive creatures

By layering these elements, the world becomes flexible, replayable, and full of surprises. Every object is designed to fit into the sandbox and interact with others in interesting ways.

Procedural Generation: Infinite Variety, Modular Design

To keep the game fresh and replayable, I built a vertical, randomly generating, infinite level system. The world is constructed from modular maps, each categorized by biome, shape, and difficulty, and slotted together based on player progression.

Modular Map System

As a solo developer, I needed a way to create massive levels without handcrafting every inch. By breaking the world into modular maps, I can mix and match pieces to create endless variety. Each map is tagged by biome, shape, and difficulty, and the system chooses which to spawn based on the player's height and progress.

  • Maps are categorized and slotted together like puzzle pieces
  • Randomness ensures every run feels fresh
  • Subsystems for objects, creatures, and maps allow for easy expansion and configuration
Maps are organized by biome, shape, and difficulty for endless variety.

Randomness & Freshness

The biggest thing that helped the game feel fresh every time was the randomness of map spawning. Every object, creature, and map is built with subsystems that can be configured, making everything fit together like a puzzle piece. This lets me create new content quickly and keep the sandbox feeling alive.

Boss Battles & Event Maps: Designing for Challenge and Surprise

Event maps and boss battles are designed to break up the flow and challenge the player in new ways, reflecting the core mechanics of SlimeClimb.

Boss battle event map: survive by climbing and using your abilities wisely.

Dynamic Events & Boss Design

  • Event maps can appear after certain milestones (e.g., after 2000m), adding surprise and variety.
  • Bosses force players to use all their skills, especially the unique mechanics they've discovered.
  • Players must dodge hazards or use their explosion ability strategically to survive.

Boss battles are a reflection of the game's core: climbing and adapting. By making event maps and bosses modular, I can easily add new content and keep the game feeling fresh for players.

Looking Forward: Expanding the Sandbox

With the core systems in place, SlimeClimb is ready to grow. Future plans include expanding to PC, adding a story mode, and releasing merchandise based on the game's characters.(hopefully lol)

Every system in SlimeClimb is built to be modular and expandable. Whether it's adding new biomes, mechanics, or story content, the foundation is in place for the game to grow with its audience.
