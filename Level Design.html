Designing Levels for SlimeClimb

Background Information

SlimeClimb is a 2D action platformer avaliable on iOS, Android & Steam; the objective is to climb as high as possible.

For the case of this portfolio I'll only be covering the main version of the game, which is the mobile version, a portrait only game.

To summarize simple mechanics of SlimeClimb, the player can jump, backflip, stick to walls, explode off the ground, and can die.


Project Information:

  • Type: Tutorial Level Design, onboarding new players through interactive learning
  • Genre: 2D vertical action platformer with physics-based movement
  • Platform Focus: Mobile-first (portrait orientation), designed for touchscreen input
  • Key Mechanics Introduced: Jumping, backflipping, wall-sticking, bounce pads, lift platforms
  • Design Goals: Teach mechanics through play, minimize player frustration, encourage exploration and choice
The tutorial level map layout for SlimeClimb, annotated to show mechanic introduction, player flow, and progression logic.

Tutorial Level - Teaching Through Play

This level introduces the player to the core mechanics of SlimeClimb in a controlled environment. Vertical progression is broken into self-contained lessons ("workshops") that each introduce a new mechanic or player concept.

  • Workshop #1: Introduces basic movement, wall-sticking, and directional control through simple platforming challenges.
  • Workshop #2: Teaches variable jump height and timing through wider gaps, emphasizing input duration and jump distance.
  • Workshop #3: Introduces backflip mechanics, bounce surfaces, and lifts in a sequence that rewards experimentation and forcably teaches the player core mechanics.
  • Workshop #4: Offers multiple paths and NPC interaction, encouraging player choice and flexible problem-solving approaches.

Each area is clearly telegraphed and structured to prevent player from being overwhelmed while subtly reinforcing newly learned mechanics.

Workshop #1 - The Basics of Jumping

The player begins on a series of basic platforms spaced closely enough that jumping between them requires only a single tap. The goal is to make movement feel safe and simple, allowing the player to naturally explore wall-sticking, sliding speeds, and directional influence.

Workshop #1: The Basics of Jumping

Workshop #2 - Variable Jump Height

The level begins to challenge the player with wider gaps. Now, holding the jump button becomes necessary. This section introduces jump height control and encourages discovery through trial and error.

  • Failure from short jumps creates natural teaching moments
  • Players begin to connect input duration to jump distance

Workshop #3 - Core Mechanics Combo

Faced with a floating coin and no obvious path, the player is nudged into experimentation. Backflipping mid-air becomes necessary to land on a bounce pad, which launches them upward to a new lift platform. These three elements - air control, bounce surfaces, and lifts are chained together to reinforce mechanic combinations.

  • Coin placement guides curiosity and trust in game design
  • Surprise and reward through successful backflip landing
Workshop #3: Core Mechanics Combo

Workshop #4 - Freedom and Expression

With all mechanics introduced, the final workshop opens up. Two distinct paths and an optional NPC create a sandbox for decision-making. The player can bypass or interact, explore or advance, reinforcing SlimeClimb's value of flexible problem solving in a linear structure.

  • Encourages replayability through alternate paths
  • Introduces NPC interaction as a non-essential design layer
Workshop #4: Freedom and Expression

This tutorial level acts not only as a mechanical introduction, but also as a thesis for the design philosophy behind SlimeClimb: clarity through exploration, teaching through play, and respect for player agency.

Level Structure & Generation

As a platformer game, I had so many different paths to take for SlimeClimb's levels. What I opted for was a vertical, "randomly generating", "infinite level". Since the goal is to climb as high as you can things have to be scaling and changing as the player climbs.

Modular Map System

It would be extremely time consuming as a solo dev to make a level as big as most players expect so instead I chopped down the level into maps.


Each map is made with this in mind:

  • It's set into categories (like biomes, shape and difficulty)
  • Put into a system that finds the players height
  • Depending on the biome, height and difficulty it's chosen to be spawned in on top of a already spawned map
Screenshot of a map, inside you can see a variety of different objects the player can interact with.

Randomness & Freshness

The biggest thing that helped the game feel fresh every time starting from the bottom was this randomness that came with this sort of map spawning.

What I learned most from making a system like this was to almost always, whether it's objects, creatures or maps, give them subsystems that can be configured and make everything fit in like a puzzle piece.

Event Maps & Boss Battles

While the level is technically infinite, I've implimented a slotting system that allows me to have set "event maps" to spawn at anytime, which opens even more interesting level design and progression possibilities.


With this system I was able to include locations with NPCs, story elements, and boss battles!

Boss Battle Design Philosophy

Designing a boss battle is like a reflection of the game itself. For example, if SlimeClimb is about climbing as high as you can, a boss battle for the game should be something that makes the player have to fight to keep climbing!

Boss Battle Mechanics

For the fight, I opened the arena up and expanded the players explosion mechanic as the main focal point to survive the boss.

You have to either dodge the falling spikeballs or backflip enough to have your limited explosion ability destroy the spikeballs. Since the ability is limited there's risks in trying to destroy the projectiles making for players having to strategize a balance to live.

Event Map Integration

To get to the boss, it's event map is configured to have a chance to spawn any time after 2000m, this goes back to the "event map" system and why opening assets to be configured is super useful when adding things to your game.
